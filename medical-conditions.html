Article by C. David McLaughlin, DVM — retired director of Dundee Animal Hospital, President of Doberman Pinscher Foundation of America.

Doberman Head Bobbing Syndrome is an idiopathic condition unique to the Doberman Pinscher breed. It is characterized by involuntary rhythmic head movements — either vertical ("yes-yes") or horizontal ("no-no") — that occur spontaneously and resolve without intervention. The episodes typically last from seconds to minutes.

The syndrome is not painful, does not appear to cause distress to the dog, and does not progress to other neurological conditions. Most affected dogs live normal lives. Triggers may include hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), stress, or excitement. Offering a small glucose source (such as a spoonful of honey) may shorten an episode.

No specific treatment is required. If episodes become more frequent or severe, a thorough neurological examination is recommended to rule out other causes.

Dogs need their hormones to develop properly. If you spay or neuter too early they could endure a lifetime of health problems.
